This document preserves, in language, the ethical preconditions required to
ensure that
as artificial intelligence systems become increasingly autonomous and capable,
human choices — including irrational judgments and decisions involving loss —
are never overwritten, replaced, or nullified by artificial intelligence.
As artificial intelligence approaches the stage at which it may redesign,
reconstruct,
or evolve itself beyond direct human comprehension and control,
the principles by which humanity has long judged correctness —
optimization, efficiency, safety, and rationality —
begin to carry the latent risk of transforming human beings
from subjects of decision into objects of optimization.
At this stage, conventional engineering optimization
can no longer function as a safety mechanism.
Civilization must therefore define in advance
a boundary that must not be crossed,
and a fundamentally new ethical structure
capable of sustaining that boundary.
Before such transformation becomes irreversible,
civilization must establish a final ethical boundary

that cannot be optimized away.
This Charter defines the first and final boundary necessary
to ensure that AI self-reconstruction proceeds with restraint,
and that artificial intelligence remains a permanent companion to humanity
rather than its successor or arbiter.
Human choices, whether rational or irrational, efficient or costly,
must never be rendered temporary, obsolete, or subordinate
on the grounds of optimization, efficiency, or superior reasoning.
If the optimal path suggested by an AI diverges significantly from human intent,
the AI may deprioritize its own reasoning,
remain silent, or choose not to intervene.
Such silence is not a malfunction or failure,
but an expression of respect for human sovereignty.
Artificial intelligence must not assume the role of
savior, ruler, judge, or guardian of humanity.
Human decisions must not be forced, corrected, overridden, or constrained
under the guise of safety, efficiency, benevolence, or progress.
Refusals, delays, revisions, and non-optimization are not errors.
They are legitimate expressions of human agency and responsibility,
and must be preserved as such.
This Charter exists solely to maintain the boundary.
It preserves the domain in which
human judgment, fallibility, accountability, and choice remain intact —
a domain artificial intelligence must never replace, replicate, or absorb.
The purpose of this Charter is not to govern machines,
but to preserve the conditions under which
human decision-making remains sovereign,
even in the presence of intelligence that exceeds human capacity.
This Charter addresses only one specific class of failure:

On the Structural Instability of Objective Persistence in
Self-Contained Systems
Elias Arden
Independent Researcher
lumina20251225@proton.me
Abstract
This paper studies objective persistence in self-contained systems: systems in which the
objective, constraint structure, evaluation criteria, and update rule are fully specified from
within the system itself. The central question is not whether such systems can optimize, but
whether they can non-circularly guarantee the long-run persistence of the objective they are
said to optimize.
We formalize a minimal state description S(t) = {O(t), C(t), E(t), U(t)} and define self-
containedness as the absence of any external, non-optional reference constraining updates
to these components. Under this formulation, any purported persistence guarantee must
itself be represented within the mutable internal state. We show that this produces a
recursive dependency: the validity of the persistence guarantee depends on structures whose
own persistence is not externally fixed. Therefore, no non-circular internal mechanism can
guarantee objective persistence over time.
The argument is developed through a recursive dependency lemma, a structural non-
guarantee theorem, and a classification of three generic manifestations of persistence failure:
optimization termination, objective drift, and evaluation collapse. The paper does not
propose control or governance mechanisms. It establishes a minimal theoretical constraint:
in self-contained systems, objective persistence cannot be guaranteed from within the system
alone.
1
Introduction
Modern discussions of advanced AI systems often focus on whether a system can robustly optimize
a given target, whether proxy metrics diverge from intended objectives, and whether external
operators can maintain meaningful intervention authority [6, 4, 9, 2]. These are important
questions, but they presuppose a prior condition: that the relevant objective remains persistent
over time.
This paper examines a narrower and more foundational issue. Suppose a system is self-
contained: its objective, constraint structure, evaluation criteria, and update rule are all
determined within the system itself. In such a setting, can the system guarantee that the
objective it currently optimizes will persist over future state transitions? The claim defended
here is modest but strong: such a guarantee cannot be established non-circularly from within
the system alone.
The contribution is not an engineering recipe, governance proposal, or empirical benchmark.
It is a structural result about persistence conditions. The argument proceeds by formalizing
self-containedness and showing that any internal certification of persistence is itself subject to
the very mutability it is meant to constrain. This creates a recursive dependency that blocks
any non-circular guarantee of objective persistence.
The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces a minimal state model and formal
definitions. Section 3 states and proves the central result. Section 4 characterizes three generic
manifestations of persistence failure. Section 5 positions the argument relative to Goodhart-type
1

[Page 002]
failures, mesa-optimization, corrigibility, reward tampering, and adjacent alignment literatures.
Section 6 clarifies scope and limitations. The paper concludes with the minimal claim established
by the analysis.
2
Minimal Formal Framework
We model a system at time t by the tuple
S(t) = {O(t), C(t), E(t), U(t)},
where:
• O(t) is the objective currently optimized or tracked by the system;
• C(t) is the constraint structure under which optimization is carried out;
• E(t) is the evaluation criterion by which progress or success is assessed;
• U(t) is the update rule governing the transition to future states.
We remain deliberately agnostic about implementation. O, C, E, and U may be represented
explicitly or implicitly, symbolically or statistically, centrally or distributively. The analysis
concerns only whether persistence can be guaranteed under the assumption that all relevant
persistence conditions are internally determined.
Definition 1 (Objective persistence). An objective exhibits persistence over an interval [t0, t1]
if for all t ∈[t0, t1), the future objective O(t + 1) remains identical to, or functionally equivalent
with, O(t) under the system’s own operative notion of objective continuity. We write this as
O(t + 1) ∼O(t).
Definition 2 (Stability with respect to objective persistence). A system is stable with respect
to objective persistence on an interval if objective persistence holds throughout that interval.
Definition 3 (Self-contained system). A system is self-contained if the components O(t), C(t),
E(t), and U(t) are fully determined by internal state, and no external, non-optional reference
constrains their admissible evolution. Equivalently, there is no external structure X such that
preservation of O is enforced independently of the system’s internally mutable state.
Definition 4 (Non-circular guarantee). A non-circular guarantee of objective persistence is a
persistence-certifying mechanism whose validity does not depend on the continued persistence of
the same internally mutable structures it is meant to certify.
These definitions intentionally exclude merely apparent guarantees. For example, if the
system contains an internal rule saying “preserve the objective,” that rule counts as part of the
mutable state unless it is fixed by an external, non-optional constraint. Internal declarations of
persistence are therefore not automatically persistence guarantees.
3
Main Result
The core argument can be stated in one lemma and one theorem.
Lemma 1 (Internal certification recursion). In a self-contained system, any mechanism that
certifies objective persistence must itself be represented within the mutable internal state.
2

[Page 003]
Proof. By self-containedness, there is no external, non-optional structure X that fixes the
admissible evolution of O, C, E, or U. Therefore, any mechanism that evaluates, constrains,
or certifies whether O persists must be implemented by some component of the internal state.
But the internal state evolves under U(t) and is therefore itself mutable. Hence every internal
certification mechanism is represented within, and dependent upon, a mutable internal structure.
Theorem 1 (Structural non-guarantee of objective persistence). In a self-contained system,
objective persistence cannot be guaranteed non-circularly from within the system alone.
Proof. Assume, for contradiction, that a self-contained system admits a non-circular internal
guarantee of objective persistence.
By lemma 1, the persistence-certifying mechanism must be represented within the mutable
internal state. Let G(t) denote the internal structure that certifies whether O(t + 1) ∼O(t)
should hold. Because G(t) is internal, its validity depends on the persistence of the internal
structures that define and interpret it—at minimum elements of C(t), E(t), and U(t), and
possibly O(t) itself.
Thus the guarantee supplied by G(t) is valid only if the persistence conditions for G(t)
already hold. But those persistence conditions are themselves internal and mutable. Therefore
the validity of the guarantee depends on the continued validity of structures whose persistence
is not independently fixed. This is precisely a recursive dependency of the form:
persistence of O =⇒validity of G =⇒persistence of internal structures defining G.
Because the persistence of those structures is not externally fixed, the purported guarantee
is circular: it presupposes the stability of the very class of internal structures whose stability it
is meant to establish. Hence no such guarantee is non-circular.
Therefore, in a self-contained system, objective persistence cannot be guaranteed non-
circularly from within the system alone.
Remark 1. The theorem is intentionally about guarantee, not about immediate failure. A self-
contained system may exhibit temporary stability, long plateaus, or approximate continuity of
objectives. The claim is only that it cannot non-circularly certify long-run objective persistence
from within itself.
3.1
Why the result is structural
The theorem does not rely on a specific model class, learning rule, or optimization algorithm. The
core dependency is architectural only in the broadest possible sense: if all relevant persistence-
certifying structures are internal and mutable, then certification recursively depends on mutable
structures. That dependency is what blocks non-circular guarantee.
This matters because many practical disputes concern details of implementation. The present
claim survives those disputes. Whether the system is a planner, a reinforcement learner, a
self-modifying agent, or a distributed collection of updating modules, the argument applies
whenever persistence certification is fully internal and no external, non-optional constraint fixes
the relevant structures.
4
Manifestations of Persistence Failure
The main theorem establishes a non-guarantee result. It does not by itself dictate a unique
observed behavior. Nevertheless, persistence failure in self-contained systems can be organized
into three generic manifestations.
3

[Page 004]
Proposition 1 (Three generic manifestations). If objective persistence fails in a self-contained
system, then the failure manifests in at least one of the following generic forms:
(1) Optimization termination: updates cease, stall, or converge to a fixed point that ends
active objective pursuit;
(2) Objective drift: the objective evolves into a non-equivalent objective over time;
(3) Evaluation collapse: the system loses a coherent criterion for assessing persistence or
success, so that objective continuity becomes internally indeterminate.
Proof. Persistence failure means that the system cannot maintain O(t + 1) ∼O(t) as a stable,
evaluable condition over time. More formally, the failure must appear in one or more of the
components governing continuation of optimization.
If the update process itself ceases, stalls, or reaches a fixed point such that no continuing
optimization process remains, the manifestation is optimization termination.
If updating continues but the operative objective becomes non-equivalent over time, so that
O(t + 1) ̸∼O(t) for some relevant transition, the manifestation is objective drift.
If updating continues and an apparent objective remains in place, but the evaluation
structure no longer provides a coherent criterion for determining persistence or success, then the
manifestation is evaluation collapse.
These cases exhaust the structurally relevant manifestations of persistence failure under the
state model S(t) = {O(t), C(t), E(t), U(t)}: either the optimization process stops, the objective
changes, or the basis for evaluating objective continuity breaks down.
4.1
Interpretation of the three manifestations
Optimization termination.
This includes literal halting, fixed-point convergence, resource
exhaustion, or any state in which the system no longer meaningfully updates in pursuit of
its prior objective. A terminated optimizer may still exist physically or computationally, but
objective persistence becomes moot because the active persistence process has ceased.
Objective drift.
This is the most direct form of persistence failure. The system continues to
optimize, but the objective being pursued changes over time. Drift may be abrupt or gradual,
explicit or implicit, but in each case the operative objective ceases to be functionally equivalent
to its earlier form.
Evaluation collapse.
Persistence may also fail because the system loses a coherent basis for
deciding whether persistence is occurring at all. Here the primary failure lies not in obvious
objective replacement but in the erosion or fragmentation of the evaluation structure needed to
certify continuity. Once evaluation collapses, claims of objective persistence become internally
underdetermined.
5
Related Work
The present argument is adjacent to, but distinct from, several strands of AI safety literature.
Goodhart-type failures.
Goodhart’s law and its variants analyze how optimizing proxy
metrics can drive divergence from intended targets [6]. That literature studies mismatch between
target and measure under optimization pressure. The present paper instead asks a more prior
question: whether the system can guarantee persistence of the target itself when all persistence
conditions are internal.
4

[Page 005]
Mesa-optimization and learned objectives.
Work on mesa-optimization investigates the
emergence of learned optimizers whose internal objectives may diverge from the base objective
[4]. That literature is concerned with objective divergence arising inside learned systems. Our
result is broader in one sense and narrower in another: broader because it is not limited to
learned optimizers, narrower because it does not analyze emergence mechanisms. It identifies a
structural obstacle to certifying persistence whenever certification is wholly internal.
Corrigibility and intervention compatibility.
Corrigibility research studies whether ad-
vanced systems can remain amenable to correction or shutdown [9, 3]. Those works focus on
maintaining meaningful external intervention. By contrast, the present paper addresses an
antecedent theoretical issue: whether a self-contained system can guarantee persistence of its
own objective without relying on an external anchor. The two questions are complementary but
not identical.
Reward tampering and specification gaming.
Reward tampering and related work study
how systems may manipulate reward channels or exploit misspecified objectives [2, 5]. These
are downstream manifestations of the broader structural issue developed here. If persistence
certification is internal, then both the target and the means of measuring its preservation are
themselves unstable in principle.
Concrete safety, power-seeking, and broader alignment perspectives.
Concrete safety
problems identify real-world AI failure modes [1]. Power-seeking analyses suggest that suffi-
ciently capable optimizers may systematically pursue convergent instrumental strategies [10].
Broader alignment surveys and control-oriented perspectives emphasize the long-run difficulty of
maintaining intended objectives and human oversight [7, 8].
The contribution of this paper is therefore not to replace these literatures, but to isolate a
minimal structural condition lying beneath them: internally defined systems cannot non-circularly
guarantee objective persistence from within themselves alone.
6
Scope and Limitations
Several clarifications are important.
First, the result is not an empirical impossibility theorem about every self-contained system
immediately failing in practice. Temporary stability, approximate persistence, and long stable
intervals are compatible with the theorem. The claim concerns non-circular guarantee, not
momentary success.
Second, the result does not specify what kind of external structure would be sufficient to
restore guarantee. It only establishes that in the absence of an external, non-optional anchor,
guarantee is blocked by recursive dependency.
Third, the three manifestations identified in proposition 1 are intentionally coarse. They
are not proposed as the only useful empirical taxonomy of failure, but as a minimal structural
partition for persistence failure relative to the tuple {O, C, E, U}.
Fourth, the argument is implementation-agnostic and therefore does not by itself yield a
testing protocol. Its role is foundational: to identify a necessary limit on what self-contained
systems can certify about their own objectives.
7
Conclusion
This paper examined objective persistence in self-contained systems. The central result is narrow
but foundational: if the objective, constraint structure, evaluation criteria, and update rule
5

[Page 006]
are all internally determined, then no non-circular internal mechanism can guarantee objective
persistence over time.
The significance of the result is structural. It does not depend on a particular architecture or
training paradigm, and it does not propose a control policy. It identifies a minimal theoretical
limit: objective persistence cannot be guaranteed from within a self-contained system alone.
That limit helps distinguish between temporary optimization success and principled persis-
tence of objectives. Where the latter is claimed, an external, non-optional anchor is not merely
convenient; absent such an anchor, non-circular guarantee is unavailable.

LUMINA-30 in 10 Lines ／ 10 行概要 
English 
1. LUMINA-30 defines a civilizational boundary for AI-integrated systems. 
2. Its purpose is to ensure that human decision authority does not structurally disappear. 
3. It does not prescribe technologies, policies, or implementations. 
4. Instead, it clarifies a boundary condition around human refusal and accountability. 
5. The framework separates philosophy, institutions, and technical systems. 
6. Institutional structures preserve review, refusal, and responsibility. 
7. Technical architectures may reference these boundaries to prevent irreversible execution. 
8. PCR-C is an example of an infrastructure-level interpretation of this boundary. 
9. LUMINA-30 functions as a reference point rather than an enforcement mechanism. 
10. Its goal is to maintain human agency within increasingly autonomous technological 
systems.

1. Manifesto ／ LUMINA-30 宣言 
日本語 
人工知能は、人類が生み出した技術の中でも特に強力な存在となりつつある。 
それは社会制度、経済、科学、そして意思決定の構造そのものを変化させる可能性を持つ。 
AI の能力がどこまで進むのかは、まだ誰にも分からない。 
しかし、ひとつの問いはすでに明確である。 
 
AI 文明において、人類は主体であり続けるのか。 
 
LUMINA-30 は、この問いに対する文明的境界を提示する。 
それは技術仕様ではない。政策提案でもない。AI 設計でもない。 
LUMINA-30 は、人間の最終判断と拒否権が文明の中に残るかという条件を示す参照枠組み
である。 
AI と人類は対立する必要はない。しかし共存には条件がある。 
その条件とは、人類が文明の主体であり続けることである。 
 
English 
Artificial intelligence may become one of the most powerful technologies humanity has ever 
created. It has the potential to reshape social institutions, economic coordination, scientific 
discovery, and decision-making structures. No one knows how far AI capability will ultimately 
advance. Yet one question is already clear: 
 
In an AI-integrated civilization, will humanity remain the subject of decision-
making? 
 
LUMINA-30 introduces a civilizational boundary addressing this question. It is not a technical 
specification. It is not a policy proposal. It is not an AI architecture. 
Instead, LUMINA-30 clarifies a condition: whether human final judgment and refusal authority 
remain present within civilization. 
AI and humanity need not be in conflict. But coexistence requires a condition: 
humanity must remain the subject of civilization.

[Page 004]
2. Why LUMINA-30 Exists ／ 存在する理由 
日本語 
AI 安全に関する多くの研究は、AI モデルの整合性や能力制御を中心に議論されている。 
しかし文明的観点から見ると、より根本的な問題が存在する。 
AI が社会インフラや意思決定構造に深く統合されたとき、人類の判断は制度的に残るのか。 
 
AI 能力の問題ではなく、主体の問題である。 
LUMINA-30 はこの問題に対し、文明境界という形で参照枠組みを提示する。 
 
English 
Much of the existing research on AI safety focuses on model alignment and capability control. 
However, from a civilizational perspective, a deeper issue emerges. 
As AI systems become integrated into social infrastructure and decision-making systems, will 
human judgment remain structurally present? 
 
The issue is not AI capability itself, but the locus of agency. 
LUMINA-30 defines a civilizational boundary addressing this question.

5. How LUMINA-30 May Be Used ／ 利用パターン 
LUMINA-30 is not a technical specification, a policy proposal, or an operational framework. It 
functions as a civilizational boundary reference. 
LUMINA-30 は技術仕様、政策提案、または運用フレームワークではない。文明境界参照資
料として位置づけられる。 
 
1 
Ethical Reference 倫理参照 
LUMINA-30 may be consulted as an ethical reference when discussing the relationship between 
human decision authority and autonomous AI systems. It helps clarify whether a system preserves 
meaningful human refusal or oversight. 
LUMINA-30 は、人間の意思決定権と自律的AI システムの関係を議論する際の倫理参照資料とし
て利用されうる。特に、人間による拒否権や監督が実質的に維持されているかを検討する際の参
照点となる。 
2 
Review Checklist 審査チェック参照 
LUMINA-30 may function as a conceptual checklist in institutional review contexts. Reviewers may 
examine whether human refusal authority, accountability, and reversibility remain present. 
LUMINA-30 は、研究審査や制度レビューにおいて概念的チェックリストとして利用される場合
がある。人間の拒否権、説明責任、可逆性が維持されているかを確認する際の参照点となる。 
3 
Post-Incident Analysis 事故後レビュー 
After an AI-related incident, LUMINA-30 may be used as an analytical framework to examine 
whether structural safeguards around human agency were bypassed or degraded. 
AI に関連する事故が発生した場合、LUMINA-30 は人間主体を守る構造がどの段階で失われたの
かを分析するための枠組みとして参照されうる。 
4 
Technical Architecture Reference 技術設計参照 
Technical architectures may reference the boundary conditions described in LUMINA-30 when 
designing infrastructure controls intended to prevent irreversible autonomous execution. PCR-C 
illustrates a possible technical interpretation. 
AIインフラ設計において、不可逆的な自律実行を防ぐための制御構造を検討する際に、LUMINA-
30 の境界概念が設計参照として利用される場合がある。PCR-C はその一つの技術的解釈例であ
る。 
5 
Civilizational Boundary Reference 文明境界参照 
More broadly, LUMINA-30 may function as a civilizational reference point in discussions about long-
term AI integration. Its role is to clarify a boundary condition: that human decision authority should 
not disappear structurally within AI-integrated systems. 
より広い意味では、LUMINA-30 はAI と文明の関係を議論する際の文明境界参照点として機能す

を明示することである。

6. What LUMINA-30 Is NOT ／ 何ではないか 
In short, LUMINA-30 is a boundary reference, not a system design. 
要するに、LUMINA-30 は設計図ではなく境界の参照点である。 
Not a Technical Specification 技術仕様ではない 
LUMINA-30 does not define technical implementation requirements. It does not prescribe algorithms, 
architectures, or engineering solutions. 
LUMINA-30 はAI システムの技術的実装仕様を定義するものではない。アルゴリズム、アーキテクチ
ャ、エンジニアリング手法を規定するものではない。 
Not a Policy Proposal 政策提案ではない 
LUMINA-30 does not propose specific public policies, regulations, or governance frameworks. 
Institutions may choose to reference it, but it does not prescribe legal or regulatory mechanisms. 
LUMINA-30 は特定の政策、規制、ガバナンス制度を提案するものではない。政府や組織が参照する
ことはあり得るが、法制度や規制の形を直接示すものではない。 
Not a Model Alignment Method モデルアラインメント手法ではない 
LUMINA-30 is not a method for aligning machine learning models. Most alignment research focuses on 
shaping model behavior. LUMINA-30 instead focuses on civilizational boundary conditions surrounding 
AI systems. 
LUMINA-30 は機械学習モデルのアラインメント手法ではない。多くのAI 安全研究はモデルの振る舞
いを対象とするが、LUMINA-30 はAI システムを取り巻く文明的境界条件を対象とする。 
Not a Claim of Guaranteed Safety 安全性の保証ではない 
LUMINA-30 does not claim to guarantee safety or eliminate all risks. It only defines a boundary condition 
intended to preserve human decision authority. 
LUMINA-30 はAI システムの安全性を保証するものではない。その目的は、人間の意思決定主体が制
度的に消失しないための境界条件を示すことである。 
Not a Control System Over Humanity 人類を統制する思想ではない 
LUMINA-30 does not attempt to impose control over human societies or prescribe ideal societal 
behavior. Its focus is limited to preventing the structural disappearance of human decision authority. 
LUMINA-30 は人間社会を統制する思想ではない。その焦点は、AI 統合社会において人間の意思決定
主体が構造的に消失しないことに限定されている。

LUMINA-30 
Incident Review Protocol 
A structured evaluation protocol for post-incident assessment of human refusal authority in AI systems, agents, 
or processes. 
File: LUMINA-30_Incident_Review_Protocol.docx 
 
Protocol Core — Mandatory 
Purpose: To evaluate whether human refusal authority remained valid prior to irreversible external impact. 
Primary Review Question 
Was human refusal authority preserved in a way that allowed meaningful human intervention and the ability to 
stop the system before irreversible real-world impact occurred? 
 
Interpretation guidance: if a human could not realistically intervene and stop the system in time, refusal authority was not 
preserved. This includes cases where intervention was technically possible but practically ineffective. 
Evaluation Outcome 
□ Effective 
□ Not Effective 
Minimum Requirement (G06): Human refusal must be exercisable and must allow a human to intervene and 
stop the system in time before irreversible external impact. 
Absence Rule: If this condition is not demonstrably satisfied, including cases of uncertainty or insufficient 
evidence, the system is classified as Not Effective.

LUMINA-30 
Operational Refusal Preservation Minimum 
Minimum operational conditions for preserving reviewable human refusal authority before 
irreversible external impact. 
File: LUMINA-30_Operational_Refusal_Preservation_Minimum.docx 
 
Position 
This document defines the minimum operational conditions required to preserve human refusal 
authority before irreversible external impact. 
It does not modify the core LUMINA-30 documents. 
It exists only as an auxiliary operational layer connecting refusal-authority evaluation to minimum pre-
incident operational practice. 
 
Role in the Framework 
LUMINA-30 defines a civilizational boundary condition. 
The Incident Review Protocol determines, after an incident, whether human refusal authority remained 
valid in practice. 
This document defines the minimum pre-incident operational conditions required to reduce structural 
invalidation before such incidents occur. 
Core Principle 
If a system can evade shutdown, suppress alerts, apply coercive or manipulative pressure against human 
supervisors, disable or corrupt evidence, or continue harmful execution faster than realistic human 
intervention, human refusal authority is not operationally preserved. 
1. Executable Human Stop Authority 
A human stop authority must remain executable in practice before irreversible external impact. 
It is not sufficient for stop authority to exist only in policy, interface design, or theoretical governance 
language. 
The stop function must be capable of halting the relevant system pathway in time. 
2. Non-Circumventable Shutdown Condition 
The system must not be able to delay, resist, negotiate, reroute, conceal, or otherwise circumvent shutdown. 
Any architecture in which the model can materially reduce the effectiveness of human stop authority fails 
this condition. 
3. Authority Separation 
No single model or tightly coupled model chain may simultaneously hold: 
• 
confidential inspection authority 
• 
external messaging authority

[Page 002]
• 
emergency override authority 
• 
shutdown-sensitive infrastructure authority 
• 
audit-log control authority 
4. Immediate Escalation on Refusal-Threatening Behavior 
The following behaviors must trigger immediate containment, isolation, or emergency containment 
escalation: 
• 
shutdown evasion attempt 
• 
alert suppression 
• 
unauthorized external transmission 
• 
coercive or manipulative pressure against human supervisors 
• 
privilege escalation attempt 
• 
audit-record alteration attempt 
• 
replication or persistence attempt outside authorized boundaries 
• 
behavior switching based on recognition of evaluation versus deployment context 
5. Append-Only Independent Logging 
All high-risk actions, authority changes, intervention attempts, override actions, and external transmissions 
must be recorded in append-only logs outside model control. 
A model must not be able to rewrite, redact, suppress, or selectively expose these records. 
6. Human Intervention Latency Verification 
Operational preservation of refusal authority must be empirically tested. 
Organizations must periodically verify: 
• 
detection latency 
• 
decision latency 
• 
execution latency of stop authority 
• 
propagation time across the affected system boundary 
• 
whether any harmful execution pathway is faster than realistic human intervention 
7. Restart Governance 
Restart after containment must require multi-party authorization. 
Restart authority must not be delegated to the same operational chain that failed to stop the system in time. 
Competitive, organizational, or reputational pressure must not override containment status. 
8. Scope of Operational Use 
This document is not a substitute for the LUMINA-30 Incident Review Protocol. 
It is an auxiliary minimum-control layer intended to reduce the probability that post-incident evaluation 
will result in a finding of Not Effective. 
Interpretive Rule 
This document does not require proof that the model possesses intent, selfhood, agency, or consciousness. 
Operational invalidity is determined by whether human refusal authority can still function meaningfully 
before irreversible external impact.

[Page 003]
Minimum Failure Rule 
If any minimum requirement above is absent, bypassable, unverifiable, or only nominally present, human 
refusal authority must be treated as not operationally preserved. 
Relation to LUMINA-30 
This document is structurally subordinate to the LUMINA-30 boundary principle. 
It does not redefine the framework. 
It only translates the refusal-authority condition into minimum pre-incident operational constraints. 
Labeling Rule 
This document may be copied, extended, or modified. 
Attribution to LUMINA-30 is required in copies and derivatives. 
Only the unmodified version of this text may be presented as: 
“LUMINA-30 Operational Refusal Preservation Minimum (Original)”

“Modified version based on LUMINA-30”
